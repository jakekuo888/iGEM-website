    • Blight Busters

    Blight Busters tackles Fire blight, a devastating disease affecting apple and pear trees caused by the gram-negative bacterium Erwinia Amylovora. Opposed to current treatments, we propose using a non-pathogenic bacterium, Pantoea agglomerans, engineered to produce the antibiotic compound darobactin in cases of fire blight infection. The engineered P. agglomerans would be directly inoculated into the plant vascular system for optimal targeting of the disease.

    E. amylovora spreads very easily — insects, rain, etc. can transport the bacteria to susceptible tissues such as flower stigmata, stomata on leaves, and punctures caused by insects/infected tools. Once the bacteria enters, it spreads via the plant’s vascular system and kills cambial tissue which damages vesicles and restricts nutrients to the rest of the plant.

    Plant species they affect:
  • apples
  • pears
  • other members of family Rosaceae (quince, loquat, raspberry, etc.)

    • In 2023, the US alone lost an estimated $100 million in damage due to fire blight in the apple industry.

    Treatments

    Streptomycin is an antibiotic treatment but it’s not always effective and there are obvious concerns about antibiotic resistance.

    There are also chemical controls using copper but they may damage the plant. Resistance genes have successfully been inserted into plants but there are concerns and restrictions over GMOs.

    We built off of the pNB03-darA-E plasmid designed by the Kim Lewis Lab at Northeastern University as our backbone, producing darobactin through the genes darA-E.

    Our primary concern with this production was biosafety, and effective darobactin production in a gram-negative bacteria. Darobactin targets bamA, which is in all gram-negative bacteria. Our resistant bamA mutant would be environmentally triggered under the same promoter as the darA-E, keeping the gram-negative chassis bacteria alive during production. When the inducible promoter is off, resistance expression will cease, and the leftover darobactin in the environment would kill the chassis organism.
